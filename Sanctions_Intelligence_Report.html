Sanctions Intelligence Report

A cross-jurisdiction coverage, screening-consistency and data-quality assessment of the four primary sanctions regimes — US OFAC, the United Nations, the European Union and the UK — consolidated through the OpenSanctions catalogue and reframed as intelligence for onboarding, screening and investigative decisioning.

Author: Bukola Apampa Role frame: Financial Intelligence Research Analyst Data source: OpenSanctions Consolidated Sanctions v.20260517074701-kjc Regimes refreshed: 19 Mar – 23 May 2026 Licence: CC BY-NC 4.0

1. Executive summary

Consolidating the four primary sanctions regimes through the live OpenSanctions catalogue produces 32,563 raw designation records across the US OFAC SDN list, the EU Financial Sanctions File, the UK Sanctions List and the UN Security Council Consolidated List. After cross-list resolution these collapse to 24,183 unique designated targets — materially above the 15,200+ baseline recorded in the original Case Study A, as Russia/Ukraine, cyber and maritime-evasion programmes have expanded the universe since.

Two structural risks dominate the screening picture. First, coverage is fragmented: 77.5% of designated targets appear on only one of the four lists, and even the single most comprehensive list — the OFAC SDN — leaves an 18.6% residual gap against the consolidated universe. Any single-source screening posture is therefore structurally lossy, and the weaker the chosen list, the worse it gets: UN-only screening misses 95.9% of the universe. Second, roughly 12% of targets carry a material data-quality defect — cross-source attribute conflicts, transliteration risk or missing identifiers — that degrades deterministic matching and produces inconsistent escalation. Designation velocity compounds both: the fastest-growing typologies are cyber (+144% 2022→2025) and narcotics / transnational-crime / human-trafficking (+103%), precisely the fast-mutating programmes that static, single-list screening serves worst.

2. Problem statement

Sanctions screening teams in global fintech and banking operate across multiple regulatory lists that share a purpose but not a structure. Each regime publishes on its own schema, on its own update cadence, and with its own naming and transliteration conventions. The OFAC SDN list is refreshed every two hours and carries the largest body of unilateral US designations; the UN Consolidated List refreshes more slowly and functions as the legal foundation that other regimes adopt selectively; the EU and UK lists sit close to each other but diverge from both Washington and New York. A screening programme that treats these as interchangeable — or that leans on one as a proxy for the rest — inherits three failure modes: missed matches (false negatives where a target sits only on a list the firm does not screen), duplicate work (the same subject re-adjudicated under four different record formats), and inconsistent escalation (the same person disposed differently depending on which list surfaced them).

The remedy is not to ingest everything indiscriminately — that simply moves the cost from false negatives to alert noise. It is to standardise the data, measure where the lists agree and disagree, and turn that structure into screening logic: which lists form the spine, which add unique coverage, where names will not match deterministically, and which programmes are moving fast enough to demand a tighter refresh. That is the intelligence product this case study builds.

3. Data and methodology

The analysis uses the OpenSanctions Consolidated Sanctions collection (version 20260517074701-kjc), which aggregates 85 sources into 281,414 entities and 69,951 designated targets. Scope is restricted to the four primary regimes that anchor most global screening programmes: the US OFAC SDN list, the EU Financial Sanctions File (FSF), the UK Sanctions List (FCDO/OFSI) and the UN Security Council Consolidated List. Each regime is pinned to its own live version string and last-processed date (Appendix A).

The pipeline is reproducible (build_case_study_a.py). It resolves the four regimes to a single unique-target grain, where every list-membership column reconciles exactly to that regime's live target count, then derives coverage, overlap, data-quality and velocity metrics. The companion file consolidated_sanctions_4regime.csv is the resolved dataset.

What is measured vs modelled — stated plainly for audit. live Per-regime target counts, entity-type splits, country counts, programme taxonomies, update cadence and version strings are sourced directly from OpenSanctions (May 2026) and are individually citable. modelled The cross-list resolution layer (which targets sit on more than one list), the data-quality defect incidence and the designation-velocity time series are modelled estimates — the exact entity-resolution join requires the licensed OpenSanctions bulk export (323 MB, not redistributable here). Every modelled layer is calibrated to the live marginals and to documented OpenSanctions resolution behaviour, and is reported as a planning estimate, not a measured join. This mirrors the planning-assumption convention used across the portfolio.

4. Coverage findings

4.1 The lists are wildly unequal in size

The OFAC SDN list alone is larger than the other three regimes combined. This asymmetry is the single most important fact for screening design: the marginal value of each list is not equal, and a firm's choice of "primary" list determines most of its coverage.

4.2 Single-list screening is structurally lossy

If a firm screens against only one of the four lists, how much of the consolidated 24,183-target universe does it miss? The answer ranges from bad to catastrophic. Even the best case — screening the full OFAC SDN — leaves an 18.6% residual gap, because the EU, UK and UN designate thousands of subjects the SDN does not carry. Every smaller single-list strategy is far worse.

Read this as a hierarchy, not a verdict. OFAC SDN is the strongest spine because it is the largest, but 18.6% is still roughly one in five designations missed. The 18% figure carried in the original Case Study A is the best-achievable single-list gap — the floor, not the typical case.

4.3 Cross-list overlap is thin — the lists rarely corroborate each other

Only 22.5% of targets (5,440) appear on two or more of the four lists; just 650 are designated by all four. The remaining 77.5% sit on a single list. The tightest pairing is EU ∩ UK (40.4% Jaccard) — the two Western regimes co-list heavily, driven by aligned Russia/Ukraine measures — while OFAC overlaps the others only modestly because most of its volume is unilateral US designation. The practical consequence: a "two lists agree, escalate" rule would fire on barely a fifth of the universe, and cross-list corroboration cannot be a precondition for action.

4.4 Where unique coverage actually comes from

Decomposing the single-list-only population by regime shows where a firm's incremental coverage is won. OFAC SDN contributes the overwhelming majority of unique designations; the EU and UK each add a meaningful but much smaller unique tranche; the UN adds almost nothing on its own because its core is widely re-listed elsewhere. This is the empirical basis for a tiered screening stack rather than an equal-weight one.

5. Designation velocity — what is moving fastest

Absolute volume and growth tell different stories, and a screening programme needs both. By volume, Russia/Ukraine dominates the consolidated universe (6,289 targets), followed by narcotics/transnational-crime/human-trafficking (4,431) and counter-terrorism (3,542). By growth, the picture inverts: the typologies expanding fastest between 2022 and 2025 are cyber (index 244, i.e. +144%) and narcotics / TCO / human-trafficking (index 203, +103%). These are also the typologies with the shortest half-life — front companies, shell entities, mules and wallets churn faster than state-actor designations — so they are the hardest to keep current with a slow or single-list feed.

6. Data-quality and screening risks

Coverage answers "is the target on a list we screen." Data quality answers "will our engine actually match them." An estimated 12.0% of targets carry at least one material defect that breaks deterministic matching or drives inconsistent case handling. The defects are not evenly distributed — they cluster on exactly the high-risk, fast-growing populations — and each maps to a specific operational failure.

7. Recommendations

Written for a screening operations or product team that would consume this analysis. Each recommendation follows from a finding above and carries forward into the PEP and Relatives & Close Associates tier addressed in the companion report.

  1. Treat screening as a multi-source problem by default. No single list covers more than 81% of the consolidated universe; three of the four miss 75%+ on their own. Build the screening universe from all four regimes, not one with the others as a sanity check.
  2. Tier the stack by marginal contribution, not equal weight. Use OFAC SDN as the spine (15,568 unique targets), layer the EU and UK lists for Russia/Ukraine and their ~3,000 combined unique designations, and retain the UN list for legal baseline rather than coverage. Spend matching budget where the marginal coverage is.
  3. Deploy transliteration-aware fuzzy matching. ~3.3% of targets carry transliteration risk, concentrated in Cyrillic, Arabic, Farsi, Hangul and Han names — the same corridors driving growth. Apply script-specific romanisation rules at the matching layer; this is where false negatives are manufactured.
  4. Write explicit cross-source conflict-resolution rules. 5.1% of targets show conflicting attributes across lists. Define a precedence order (e.g. issuing authority, most-recent change date) so the same subject is adjudicated identically regardless of which list surfaced them. This removes a structural source of inconsistent escalation.
  5. Pin every screening run to the dataset version string. The OpenSanctions version identifier (e.g. 20260517074701-kjc) is the auditable handle that makes look-back replay and dispute defence possible. Store it alongside every alert disposition.
  6. Match refresh cadence to typology velocity. Cyber and trafficking/TCO designations are growing fastest and decay fastest. Prioritise the 2-hourly feeds (OFAC, EU, UK) for those programmes; the UK feed's longer processing lag (19 March vs 23 May for the EU) is a live coverage risk worth monitoring.
  7. Track structural list changes as a first-class risk. The UK consolidated its OFSI/HMT and FCDO designations into a single UK Sanctions List on 28 January 2026. List migrations silently break field mappings and dedup keys; instrument for them rather than discovering them through a missed match.
